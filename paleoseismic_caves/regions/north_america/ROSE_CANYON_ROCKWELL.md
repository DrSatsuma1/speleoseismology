## 2. The Newport-Inglewood-Rose Canyon Cascade System

### Fault System Architecture

The [NIRCFZ extends 110+ miles](https://scedc.caltech.edu/earthquake/newportrose.html) from San Diego Bay to Culver City, running offshore for most of its length within 4 miles of the coast.

According to [Sahakian et al. (2017, JGR)](https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/2016JB013467) and coverage in [Temblor](https://temblor.net/earthquake-insights/new-study-finds-los-angeles-and-san-diego-are-at-risk-from-m7-3-offshore-earthquakes-2726/):

| Segment | Stepover | Barrier to Rupture? |
|---------|----------|---------------------|
| Rose Canyon → Offshore | <2 km | **NO** |
| Offshore → Newport-Inglewood | <2 km | **NO** |
| Newport-Inglewood → Onshore | <2 km | **NO** |

**Critical threshold**: Stepovers >3 km typically inhibit continuous rupture. All NIRCFZ stepovers are <2 km.

### Maximum Cascade Rupture Scenario

[UCSD News (2016)](https://ucsdnews.ucsd.edu/pressrelease/fault_system_off_san_diego_orange_los_angeles_counties_could_produce_a_magn):

> "A fault system that runs from San Diego to Los Angeles is capable of producing up to **magnitude 7.3 earthquakes** if the offshore segments rupture and a **7.4 if the southern onshore segment also ruptures.**"

**Impact**: An M7.3+ event would cause simultaneous severe shaking from San Diego to Los Angeles, affecting 15+ million people.

### Connection to UCERF3 and Scharer-Yule Clustering Evidence

Our Rose Canyon findings connect directly to a documented problem in California seismic hazard modeling.

**The UCERF3 Paleoseismic Misfit**: UCERF3 underestimates paleoseismic slip rates on the southern SAF by ~25%. Morgan Page (2020, USGS) demonstrated this misfit could be resolved by incorporating **greater fault connectivity** - precisely what our NIRCFZ cascade evidence supports.

**Scharer & Yule (2020) Maximum Rupture Model**: Their comprehensive paleoseismic compilation identified three clustering epochs on the southern SAF and San Jacinto Fault:

| Epoch | Events | Character |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| **~975 CE** | R38, R37, R36 | Rapid succession |
| **~1550 CE** | R17, R15, R14 | Clustered within decades |
| **1800-1857** | Historic cluster | >85% of SAF ruptured |

> "Rupture of >85% of the fault length in the historic period between 1800 and 1857 and the subsequent quiescence is similar to epochs of activity in the prehistoric model, suggesting that **regional clustering of seismicity could be a trait of the system**."

**Key Quote**:
> "The rupture pattern suggests that **cascades and overlapping events are common** and produce epochs of heightened regional clustering, yet these issues are not addressed in most earthquake scenarios or in preparedness planning."

**Our Contribution**:
1. The Crystal Cave ~1745 + Rose Canyon mid-1700s + SJF ~1750 synchronization provides **independent multi-proxy confirmation** of the cascade/clustering pattern
2. Our Rose Canyon ~1300 correlation with SAF paleoseismic windows suggests clustering extended to the NIRCFZ in medieval times
3. If NIRCFZ follows the same clustering behavior as SAF/SJF, San Diego hazard may be underestimated during cluster epochs

**References**:
- Scharer, K.M. & Yule, D. (2020). A Maximum Rupture Model for the Southern San Andreas and San Jacinto Faults. *GRL* 47(15), e2020GL088532.

## 13. NEW: The 1741 Dark Earthquake Hypothesis (2024-12-29)

### Discovery

Analysis of San Diego tree ring data reveals a **potential undocumented earthquake in ~1741 CE** - approximately 4-9 years BEFORE Rockwell's documented "mid-1700s" Rose Canyon event.

### Evidence Summary

| Proxy | Location | Distance to RC | 1741 Signal | Pattern |
|-------|----------|----------------|-------------|---------|
| Tree rings | Mt. Laguna | 55 km | z=-1.09 | **Suppression** |
| Tree rings | Palomar Mt. | 75 km | z=-1.10 | **Suppression** |
| Speleothem | Crystal Cave | 450 km | z=+2.01 | **Enhancement** |

### Critical Divergence Analysis

**San Diego trees**: SUPPRESSION (negative z-scores)
**Sequoia speleothem**: ENHANCEMENT (positive z-scores)

This **regional divergence** is the key discrimination:
- If the 1741 suppression were caused by the 1739 Tarumae volcanic eruption (Japan), we would expect UNIFORM suppression at all sites
- Instead, San Diego shows damage while Sequoia shows enhancement
- The two San Diego sites are highly CONVERGENT (spread = 0.02), indicating a shared LOCAL cause

### Volcanic False Positive Check (COMPLETED 2024-12-29)

**Potential confounding factor**: The 1739 Tarumae eruption (Japan) affected global tree rings in 1740 per [Briffa et al. 1998](https://www.nature.com/articles/33859).

**Eruption Details** (from [Global Volcanism Program](https://volcano.si.edu/volcano.cfm?vn=285040)):
- **VEI 5** (plinian, NOT VEI 4 as initially thought)
- **Volume**: 4.5 km³ total erupted material
- **Plume height**: >25 km (reached stratosphere)
- **Date**: August 1739

**Ice Core Analysis**:
- Searched [eVolv2k database](https://essd.copernicus.org/articles/9/809/2017/) (500 BCE - 1900 CE)
- **Result**: 1739 Tarumae not explicitly listed in major eruption catalogs
- The eruption may not have produced a bipolar (Greenland + Antarctic) sulfate signal
- Northern Hemisphere-only signal would have more limited global impact

**Critical Counter-Evidence: THE DIVERGENCE**

| Proxy | Location | 1741 Signal | If Volcanic | Actual |
|-------|----------|-------------|-------------|--------|
| Tree rings | San Diego | z=-1.09 | Suppression ✓ | Suppression |
| Speleothem | Sequoia | z=+2.01 | Suppression ✗ | **ENHANCEMENT** |

**The Sequoia ENHANCEMENT is the key discriminator:**
- Volcanic cooling → global suppression everywhere
- San Diego DOWN + Sequoia UP = **regional divergence**
- This pattern is consistent with LOCAL seismic damage in San Diego, NOT global volcanic forcing

**Additional Evidence**:
1. 1738 suppression at San Diego (z=-1.07) PREDATES the August 1739 eruption
2. Crystal Cave enhancement persists through 1749 (14 years) - too long for volcanic
3. Peak Crystal Cave signal at 1745 (z=+2.84) is 6 years after eruption - wrong timing for volcanic

**Conclusion**: The 1741 San Diego tree ring suppression is **unlikely to be volcanic** based on:
- Regional divergence with Sequoia
- Wrong timing (1738 predates eruption, 1745 peak too late)
- Ice core evidence for 1739 Tarumae is weak/absent

**Confidence: MODERATE** - The 1741 dark earthquake hypothesis survives volcanic discrimination, but z-scores are modest.

**Scenario A: Separate Event**
- 1741: Unknown Rose Canyon M5.5-6.0 (not captured in trenches)
- 1745-1750: Rockwell's documented M6.5+ event
- Two earthquakes in rapid succession

**Scenario B: Precursory Activity**
- 1741: Foreshock sequence or creep event
- 1745-1750: Mainshock rupture
- Tree rings captured early strain release

**Scenario C: Dating Uncertainty**
- Rockwell's "mid-1700s" radiocarbon date has ±10-20 year uncertainty
- Could actually be 1741 ± dating error
- Single event, not two

### Why This Matters

If 1741 was a separate earthquake:
1. **Recurrence refinement**: Rose Canyon may have more frequent moderate events than trenches show
2. **Pre-colonial record**: First tree-ring detection of a Rose Canyon event with NO historical documentation
3. **Cascade validation**: Did 1741 trigger the larger 1745-1750 event?

### Limitations

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| Z-score magnitude | Moderate (z=-1.09 to -1.10), weaker than 1855 (z=+2.05) |
| No written records | Pre-1769, cannot confirm with historical sources |
| Volcanic overlap | 1739 Tarumae must be ruled out via ice cores |
| Not in trenches | If real, was below detection threshold or off-fault |

## 14. The Offshore Record: Why Turbidite Paleoseismology FAILED in San Diego

### CORRECTION (2024-12-29): Researchers DID Try

**Previous assessment was incorrect.** Researchers HAVE attempted offshore turbidite paleoseismology in San Diego - and FAILED due to fundamental geological limitations.

### Key Study: USGS Report G16AP00119 (Maloney & Driscoll)

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **PIs** | Jillian Maloney (SDSU), Neal Driscoll (Scripps) |
| **Data** | 32 CHIRP seismic profiles (280 line-km) + gravity cores |
| **Targets** | Oceanside Fan, San Gabriel Canyon, La Jolla Fan |
| **Result** | **FAILED to establish event chronology** |

### Why It Failed: Storm vs. Earthquake Discrimination

From the report:
> "The paleoseismic history remains **entirely unconstrained** regarding recurrence intervals."

**The fundamental problem**: In San Diego, researchers cannot distinguish between:
- Turbidites triggered by **earthquakes**
- Turbidites triggered by **winter storms/El Niño**

**Why Cascadia works but San Diego doesn't**:

| Factor | Cascadia | San Diego |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| River systems | Multiple converging | Single isolated (La Jolla) |
| Synchronicity test | Can verify simultaneous triggering | Cannot verify |
| Bypass problem | Fan deposits preserved | La Jolla Canyon scours/erodes |
| Signal-to-noise | High (deep water) | Low (shallow, storm-dominated) |

### What They DID Establish

| Finding | Value | Source |
|---------|-------|--------|
| Slip rate | 1.2-1.5 mm/yr | Verified Ryan et al. 2012 |
| Holocene activity | Confirmed | Fault cuts upper 5-10m sediment |
| Fault length | 120 km | Connected to northern systems |

### The Spanish Bight Anomaly (SCEC Report 16195)

A potentially **unrecorded recent rupture** on the Rose Canyon system:

| Finding | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Location | Spanish Bight Fault (San Diego Bay) |
| Evidence | Fault offsets post-1950 dredge layer |
| Implication | Creep or small rupture in last ~75 years |
| Historical record | **NOTHING recorded** |

This suggests Rose Canyon may have more frequent small events than the trench record shows.

### Why This Makes Tree Rings MORE Valuable

Since offshore turbidite paleoseismology is **fundamentally limited** in San Diego:

| Proxy | Status | Value for Rose Canyon |
|-------|--------|----------------------|
| Speleothems | ❌ None (no karst) | N/A |
| Offshore turbidites | ❌ **FAILED** (can't discriminate) | Cannot provide event dates |
| Onshore trenches | ✅ Works | 4 events in 3,300 years |
| **Tree rings** | ✅ **ONLY viable alternative** | Can detect events between trench intervals |

**Our 1741 and 1855 tree ring signals may be the ONLY way to identify Rose Canyon dark earthquakes.**

## 15. FUTURE: ML Approach to Turbidite Discrimination

### The Opportunity

Maloney & Driscoll's work was completed **before modern ML techniques** became widely available. A machine learning approach could potentially solve the storm vs. earthquake discrimination problem.

### Why ML Could Work

**1. Multi-Dimensional Pattern Recognition**
Traditional methods looked at individual features. ML can combine:
- Grain size distribution curves (not just mean)
- Compositional fingerprints (mineral assemblages)
- Layer thickness vs lateral extent ratios
- Organic carbon content profiles
- Bioturbation patterns

**2. Temporal Feature Engineering**
- Storm turbidites should cluster in **El Niño years**
- Earthquake turbidites should correlate with **onshore paleoseismic dates**
- Rockwell's ~1650, ~1523 AD dates provide **training labels**

**3. Spatial Correlation (Multi-Core)**
- Earthquakes trigger turbidites **synchronously** across multiple sites
- Storms may trigger **sequentially** as they move through the region
- If multiple cores exist, this is a strong discriminator

**4. Transfer Learning from Cascadia**
- Goldfinger has **labeled earthquake turbidites** from Cascadia
- Train on Cascadia "earthquake turbidite signatures"
- Apply to San Diego as semi-supervised classification
