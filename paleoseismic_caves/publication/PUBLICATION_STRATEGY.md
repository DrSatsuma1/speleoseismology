## 12.6 Confirmation Bias and Unfalsifiability Risks

This section addresses fundamental epistemological challenges that must be explicitly acknowledged in publication. Addressing these concerns proactively strengthens rather than weakens the scientific case.

### 12.6.1 Confirmation Bias Risks

**The "Texas Sharpshooter" Problem**

Our methodology is vulnerable to the classic pattern-matching fallacy:
- We searched for geochemical anomalies in caves located near active faults during periods of suspected catalog incompleteness
- Finding anomalies where we looked does not prove they are earthquakes
- Risk of unconsciously favoring seismic interpretations over equally plausible climatic alternatives

**Specific Bias Vectors:**

| Bias Type | Description | Mitigation |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| **Selection bias** | Chose caves near faults, not random locations | Report null results (1456 Molise, 2012 Guatemala M7.4) |
| **Temporal selection** | Focused on catalog gaps, not random periods | Include full time series in supplementary data |
| **Interpretation bias** | May favor seismic interpretations for ambiguous signals | Require multi-proxy confirmation for any claim |
| **Publication bias** | Positive results more likely to be reported | Document all analyses, including failures |

**Recommendation for Paper**: Include explicit paragraph in Methods or Discussion acknowledging these biases and explaining our mitigation strategies.

### 12.6.2 Unfalsifiability Concerns

**The Core Problem**

A robust scientific hypothesis must specify conditions under which it would be falsified. Our methodology risks the following asymmetry:

- Anomaly matches earthquake date â†’ "Seismic signal detected"
- No anomaly during documented earthquake â†’ "Below detection threshold" or "Mechanism didn't apply at this site"
- This asymmetry makes the hypothesis difficult to disprove

**What Would Falsify the Methodology?**

The paper must clearly state falsification criteria:

1. **Systematic null results for documented earthquakes**: If caves near large documented earthquakes consistently show NO anomalies, the method fails. (Current evidence: **46.7% detection rate at z>=2 for Cascadia megathrusts, 66.7% including possible matches**)

2. **Identical signals from non-seismic causes**: If we cannot distinguish seismic from volcanic/climatic signals using multi-proxy criteria, the method lacks discrimination power. (Current evidence: Coupling ratios and trace elements provide discrimination)

3. **Random distribution of anomalies**: If anomaly timing is uncorrelated with seismicity, the method detects noise. (Current evidence: **P < 0.00001** for Cascadia temporal clustering - 9.3x above random chance)

**Recommendation for Paper**: Include "Falsifiability Criteria" subsection specifying what evidence would disprove the hypothesis.
