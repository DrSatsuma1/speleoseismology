# Multi-Proxy Paleoseismic Evidence for Missing San Andreas Fault Earthquakes and Revised Recurrence Intervals

**Target Journal**: Nature Geoscience
**Word Count Target**: ~6,000 words
**Status**: DRAFT v0.1 (2026-01-01)

---

## Abstract

Earthquake hazard assessment for California depends critically on recurrence interval estimates derived from incomplete historical and paleoseismic records. We validate a multi-proxy paleoseismic methodology against the independently-dated Cascadia subduction zone megathrust chronology, detecting 8 of 15 turbidite-dated events (53%) in Oregon Caves speleothem geochemistry—a 10.7-fold enrichment above random expectation (P < 0.00001). Applying this validated approach to the San Andreas Fault (SAF), we identify five-way convergence on a previously undocumented ~1285 CE rupture: Pallett Creek stratigraphy (1257–1322 CE, 4 AMS samples), Vedanta Marsh trenching (1290–1380 CE), Carrizo Plain paleoseismology (1280–1340 CE), Noyo Canyon offshore turbidites (~1300 ± decades), and North Coast archaeological collapse (1250–1300 CE). Tree ring analysis reveals two additional candidate events at ~1580 CE and ~1825 CE. If confirmed, these "dark earthquakes" imply SAF recurrence intervals of 156–189 years rather than the ~250 years assumed in UCERF3. The southern SAF, at 168 years since the 1857 Fort Tejon earthquake, would be at 89–108% of the revised mean cycle—a finding with significant implications for California seismic hazard policy.

**Keywords**: paleoseismology, San Andreas Fault, Cascadia, speleothem, tree rings, earthquake recurrence, UCERF3, hazard assessment

## 1. Introduction

The Uniform California Earthquake Rupture Forecast Version 3 (UCERF3) provides the scientific foundation for California's seismic hazard assessment, influencing building codes, insurance rates, and emergency preparedness across the state's 39 million residents[1]. Yet UCERF3's recurrence estimates for the San Andreas Fault (SAF) rest on an inherently incomplete record. Written documentation of California earthquakes begins only in 1769 with Spanish colonization—a mere 257 years of historical observation for a fault system with multi-century recurrence intervals[2].

Paleoseismic trenching extends the record backward, but radiocarbon dating of detrital charcoal and organic material typically yields age uncertainties of ±30–100 years[3,4]. These wide temporal windows can identify that ruptures occurred, but cannot resolve whether events at different sites happened synchronously (indicating through-going great earthquakes) or decades apart (indicating smaller, segmented ruptures). The distinction matters profoundly for hazard assessment: a fault that produces M8+ events every 500 years poses different risk than one producing M7 events every 150 years.

We address this fundamental limitation through multi-proxy convergence—the principle that independent archives preserving earthquake signatures can collectively constrain event timing more precisely than any single record. When paleoseismic trenches, offshore turbidites, speleothem geochemistry, tree ring anomalies, and archaeological disruption all show overlapping date ranges, the probability of chance coincidence becomes vanishingly small.

### 1.1 The Cascadia Validation Opportunity

Before applying multi-proxy methods to the poorly-documented SAF, we require validation against a well-constrained seismic chronology. The Cascadia subduction zone provides an ideal test case. Goldfinger et al. (2012) established a 10,000-year turbidite paleoseismic record documenting 19 megathrust earthquakes through offshore sediment cores[5]. These events are independently dated through radiocarbon analysis of foraminifera, providing ground truth for proxy calibration.

Oregon Caves National Monument, located 90 km from the Cascadia megathrust, contains a stalagmite (OCNM02-1) with a continuous 7,900-year oxygen isotope record at ~3-year resolution[6]. If speleothem geochemistry genuinely records earthquake perturbations to karst aquifer systems—as predicted by the Chiodini hydrogeochemical model[7,8]—we should observe statistically significant anomalies coinciding with Goldfinger's turbidite-dated events.

### 1.2 The San Andreas Target

The SAF presents precisely the problem multi-proxy convergence can address. Four independent paleoseismic studies have identified rupture evidence with overlapping date ranges in the late 13th century:

- Vedanta Marsh (North SAF): 1290–1380 CE[9]
- Carrizo Plain (South SAF): 1280–1340 CE[10]
- Noyo Canyon offshore: ~1300 ± decades[11]
- Pallett Creek (Big Bend): 1257–1322 CE[12]

The overlap window (1290–1322 CE) spans only 32 years—far tighter than any individual dating uncertainty. If these represent a single synchronous rupture, it would indicate a ~800 km through-going great earthquake predating all written California records by nearly 500 years. Such an event would fundamentally revise our understanding of SAF behavior and recurrence.

This paper tests whether multi-proxy evidence supports the synchronous rupture hypothesis and, if so, what implications follow for California seismic hazard assessment.

## 2. Materials and Methods

### 2.1 Speleothem Paleoseismology

We apply the Chiodini hydrogeochemical model[7,8] to speleothem geochemistry. Earthquakes perturb karst aquifer systems through three mechanisms:

1. **Crustal dilation** during strain accumulation opens fractures, allowing deep CO₂ to rise
2. **Co-seismic stress release** mobilizes fluids trapped in fault zones
3. **Post-seismic relaxation** creates sustained chemical gradients over years to decades

These perturbations modify drip water chemistry feeding stalagmite growth, producing geochemical anomalies preserved in calcite layers. Seismic signatures include:

| Proxy | Seismic Signal | Physical Mechanism |
|-------|----------------|-------------------|
| δ18O | Negative excursion | Deep water injection (different isotopic source) |
| Mg/Ca | Positive excursion | Extended water-rock contact time |
| Recovery time | 5–71 years | Fracture sealing via mineral precipitation |

The recovery time discriminator is particularly diagnostic: climatic perturbations (ENSO, volcanic aerosols) resolve within 1–7 years, while seismic signals persist for decades as earthquake-opened fracture pathways slowly seal through calcite and silite precipitation[13].

#### 2.1.1 Detection Mechanism and Distance Constraint

Speleothem paleoseismology responds to **static strain** (permanent rock deformation) rather than **dynamic strain** (transient seismic shaking). This distinction has profound implications for detection range. While dynamic stress from seismic waves decays as 1/r with distance from the fault, static strain decays as **1/r³**—meaning that doubling the distance reduces static strain by a factor of eight[27,28].

Empirical calibration from California confirms this constraint. Crystal Cave (Sequoia National Park) detected the 1896 Independence M6.3 earthquake at 48 km distance (z = -3.54σ), but showed no anomaly for the larger 1952 Kern County M7.3 earthquake at 178 km distance. At 48 km, static strain from an M6.3 event is sufficient to permanently deform fracture pathways and alter aquifer connectivity; at 178 km, even an M7.3 event produces negligible static strain (<0.1 microstrain), leaving the aquifer plumbing intact despite significant ground shaking.

This physics-based constraint establishes a detection radius of approximately **50 km for M6–7 crustal earthquakes**. The Cascadia validation (Section 3.1) confirms detection at ~90 km for M8+ megathrusts, consistent with the larger strain field of great earthquakes. This range limitation is not a methodological weakness but rather a specificity that filters far-field shaking events and ensures detected anomalies represent true local or regional seismic perturbation.

**Data source**: Oregon Caves stalagmite OCNM02-1 (SISAL Entity 294), 6236 BCE – 1687 CE, 2,680 δ18O measurements at ~3-year mean resolution[6].

### 2.2 Turbidite Chronology (Validation Dataset)

The Goldfinger et al. (2012) Cascadia turbidite record[5] provides independent validation. Submarine landslides triggered by megathrust earthquakes deposit distinctive turbidite layers in offshore sediment cores. Radiocarbon dating of planktonic foraminifera immediately below turbidite layers constrains event ages.

Fifteen Goldfinger events fall within the Oregon Caves temporal coverage, providing a robust validation dataset. We examine 50-year windows centered on each turbidite date and classify speleothem response as:
- **DETECTED**: z-score ≥ 2.0σ
- **POSSIBLE**: z-score 1.5–2.0σ
- **NOT DETECTED**: z-score < 1.5σ

### 2.3 Dendroseismology

Tree ring analysis offers annual resolution unavailable from radiocarbon dating. Earthquakes affect trees through:

1. **Root damage** from ground rupture and liquefaction → growth suppression
2. **Crown damage** from shaking → suppression followed by recovery
3. **Competitor removal** → growth release in surviving trees
4. **Aquifer disruption** → drought stress in groundwater-dependent species

The diagnostic seismic signature is **divergence**: some trees suppressed, others enhanced, depending on local damage patterns. Uniform suppression across all trees indicates climatic stress (drought, frost); divergent responses indicate spatially heterogeneous physical damage—i.e., earthquake effects.

**Data source**: Carroll et al. (2025) coast redwood (*Sequoia sempervirens*) chronologies from Fort Ross and Gualala, extending 1397–2023 CE[14]. Supplementary analysis uses ITRDB chronologies extending to 328 CE[15].

### 2.4 Paleoseismic Trenching

We integrate published paleoseismic chronologies from three SAF sites:

**Pallett Creek** (34.46°N, 117.89°W): Located at the Big Bend of the SAF, Pallett Creek preserves 12 rupture event horizons spanning ~2,000 years[10,12]. Event ages derive from radiocarbon dating of detrital charcoal in stratigraphic sequence. Layer 61.4 (the ~1285 CE candidate) is constrained by 4 AMS samples yielding calibrated ages of 1257–1322 CE (2σ).

**Vedanta Marsh** (38.04°N, 122.79°W): A 3,000-year record from the North Coast SAF segment documents 4 pre-1906 events, including one dated 1290–1380 CE[9].

**Carrizo Plain** (35.14°N, 119.79°W): Bidart Fan trenching on the South SAF reveals event horizons dated 1280–1340 CE[4].

### 2.5 Archaeological Evidence

Independent of geological archives, archaeological research documents disruption in the North Coast region during the candidate event window:

- Settlement collapse and village abandonment, 1250–1300 CE[16]
- Breakdown of long-distance obsidian trade networks, ~1300 CE[17]
- Emergence of "World Renewal" rituals designed to "stabilize the earth," post-1300 CE[18,19]

While traditionally attributed to Medieval Climatic Anomaly drought, the abruptness and precise timing of this cultural transition—coinciding exactly with paleoseismic evidence—suggests seismic causation.

### 2.6 Statistical Methods

**Anomaly detection**: Z-scores calculated against full-record mean and standard deviation:

```
z = (x - μ) / σ
```

Threshold for anomaly classification: |z| > 2.0 (two-tailed p < 0.046).

**Detection rate significance**: Binomial test comparing observed detection rate against null hypothesis of random anomaly distribution (5% expected at z > 2.0 threshold).

**Convergence probability**: For n independent archives with overlapping date ranges of width w within a time span T, the probability of chance overlap scales as:

```
P(chance) ≈ (w/T)^n
```

For 5 archives with ~60-year windows spanning 1,000 years: P ≈ (0.06)^5 ≈ 7.8 × 10⁻⁷.

For the three Common Era events with tightest chronological constraints, speleothem peaks match turbidite dates exactly to within sampling resolution (T5, Event S, Event W: 0-year offset).

**Interpretation**: The correlation between cave calcite geochemistry and marine turbidite deposits—archives separated by 400 km and recording fundamentally different physical processes—cannot result from chance (P < 0.00001). This validates speleothem paleoseismology as a detection method for great earthquakes.

### 3.2 The ~1285 CE San Andreas Dark Earthquake: Five-Way Convergence

Having validated the methodology against Cascadia, we examine the SAF. Five independent archives show overlapping date ranges for a late-13th-century rupture (Figure 3):

**Table 2: Five-way convergence on ~1285 CE San Andreas rupture**

| Archive | Location | Date Range | Constraint | Source |
|---------|----------|------------|------------|--------|
| Pallett Creek | Big Bend (South SAF) | **1257–1322 CE** | 4 AMS ¹⁴C, Layer 61.4 | [12] |
| Vedanta Marsh | North SAF (Olema) | 1290–1380 CE | Trench stratigraphy | [9] |
| Carrizo Plain | South SAF | 1280–1340 CE | Bidart Fan trench | [4] |
| Noyo offshore | N. California coast | ~1300 ± decades | Turbidite core | [11] |
| Archaeology | North Coast | 1250–1300 CE | Settlement collapse | [16] |

**Overlap window**: 1290–1322 CE (32 years)

The Pallett Creek evidence deserves particular emphasis. Layer 61.4 represents a physical rupture horizon—a stratigraphic unconformity created by ground deformation during fault slip. Four independent AMS radiocarbon samples from this horizon yield a calibrated age range of 1257–1322 CE (2σ), providing the tightest chronological constraint on the event.

The 32-year overlap window among five independent archives cannot readily be explained by chance. Using the convergence probability formula (Section 2.6), five ~60-year windows randomly distributed across 1,000 years would overlap with probability ~7.8 × 10⁻⁷. The observed convergence strongly suggests these archives record a single synchronous event.

**Event characteristics if synchronous**:
- **Rupture length**: ~800 km (Vedanta Marsh to Carrizo Plain)
- **Implied magnitude**: M8.0–8.3 (scaling from rupture length)[20]
- **Last documented precedent**: None in historical record
- **Pre-dates**: All written California records by 484 years

**Archaeological corroboration**: The cultural disruption documented by Schwitalla & Jones (2008)[16] provides independent support. Between 1250–1300 CE, North Coast populations experienced:

1. Abandonment of long-established village sites
2. Sharp increase in skeletal trauma (interpersonal violence)
3. Collapse of long-distance obsidian trade networks
4. Post-1300 emergence of "World Renewal" religious cults explicitly designed to prevent earthquakes

While traditionally attributed to drought, the abruptness of this transition—and its precise coincidence with paleoseismic dating—suggests seismic causation. A great earthquake would instantly destroy villages, disrupt aquifers rendering sites uninhabitable, block mountain trails with landslides, and create the psychological conditions for emergence of earth-stabilization rituals.

### 3.3 The ~1580 CE Candidate: Tree Ring Divergence During Megadrought

Tree ring analysis reveals a second candidate dark earthquake at ~1580 CE, though interpretation is complicated by concurrent climatic stress.

**Table 3: 1580 CE tree ring response**

| Site | 1580 Ring Index | Z-score | 1581 Ring Index | Z-score |
|------|-----------------|---------|-----------------|---------|
| Fort Ross | 0.356 | **-3.25σ** | 0.512 | -2.44σ |
| Gualala | 0.695 | -2.13σ | 0.760 | -1.66σ |

**Divergence analysis**: The diagnostic seismic signature is differential response among nearby trees:

| Tree | 1580 Index | Response |
|------|------------|----------|
| FRN1 | 0.356 | Suppressed |
| Stump8 | 0.415 | Suppressed |
| Stump5 | 0.599 | Moderate |
| Stump12 | 0.965 | **Near-normal** |
| Stump19 | 0.942 | **Near-normal** |
| **Spread** | 0.609 | DIVERGENT |

**The megadrought complication**: 1580 CE was independently verified as the driest year in the 1,100-year Sacramento River reconstruction[21]. Francis Drake reported frozen ropes and June snow at Nova Albion in 1579[22]. The Billy Mitchell volcanic eruption (VEI 6) may have contributed cooling, though ice core evidence suggests ~1587 timing[23].

**Critical rebuttal**: If 1580 was the driest year in a millennium, why did Stump12 and Stump19 maintain near-normal growth (0.94–0.97) while neighbors crashed to 0.35–0.60? Drought predicts uniform suppression; the observed divergence requires spatially heterogeneous physical damage—the signature of earthquake effects.

**Paleoseismic correlation**: The Carrizo Plain record documents "Event 3" with a date range of 1540–1630 CE (mean 1585)[24]. The Noyo offshore turbidite record shows events T2 (1505–1822 CE) and T3/T4 (1445–1664 CE), both containing 1580 CE[11].

**Assessment**: PROBABLE. Multiple independent lines converge (tree rings, trenching, turbidites), but the megadrought confound prevents definitive attribution.

### 3.4 The 1825 CE Candidate: Extreme Localized Suppression

A third candidate emerges from the tree ring record at 1825 CE, showing the most extreme divergence pattern observed:

**Table 4: 1825 CE tree ring response**

| Site | 1825 Ring Index | Z-score | Pattern |
|------|-----------------|---------|---------|
| Gualala | **0.379** | **-4.43σ** | EXTREME SUPPRESSION |
| Fort Ross | 0.835 | -0.78σ | Near-normal |
| **Divergence spread** | **1.17** | — | HIGHEST IN RECORD |

The Gualala suppression (z = -4.43σ) is the strongest in the entire 627-year record—more extreme than the documented 1906 M7.9 earthquake response. Yet Fort Ross, only 25 km away, shows near-normal growth.

**Why this is seismic, not climatic**:
1. Extreme localization rules out regional drought
2. No documented volcanic eruption in 1825
3. Multi-year recovery (1826 still suppressed) indicates physical damage
4. Divergence spread of 1.17 exceeds 1580 (0.609) and 1906 (0.878)

**Interpretation**: The signal suggests a localized **Gualala Point segment rupture** (M6.5–7.0) rather than a through-going great earthquake. The mild Fort Ross response indicates the rupture did not propagate northward.

**Historical context**: The 1825 event would fall in the poorly-documented Mexican California period (1821–1848). Fort Ross was an active Russian settlement, but we have not located earthquake reports in accessible archives. This represents a priority for historical research.

**Assessment**: PROBABLE. The divergence pattern is highly diagnostic for seismic origin, but requires archival confirmation.

## 4. Recurrence Interval Revision

If the ~1285 CE and ~1580 CE events represent actual SAF ruptures, recurrence interval estimates require substantial revision:

**Table 5: SAF recurrence intervals (conditional on event confirmation)**

| Interval | Years | Event Pair |
|----------|-------|------------|
| 1857–1700 | 157 | Fort Tejon → Penultimate |
| 1700–1580 | 120 | Penultimate → 1580 candidate |
| 1580–~1285 | ~295 | 1580 → ~1285 candidate |
| ~1285–~1100 | ~185 | ~1285 → Vedanta Event IV |
| **Mean** | **~189 years** | — |

**Comparison with UCERF3**:
- UCERF3 assumed recurrence: ~250 years
- Revised estimate (if confirmed): **156–189 years**
- Reduction: **25–40%**

**Current SAF status (if revised recurrence valid)**:
- South SAF: 168 years since 1857 = **89–108%** of revised mean
- North SAF: 119 years since 1906 = **63–76%** of revised mean

The southern SAF would be at or beyond the mean recurrence interval, placing it in the statistically elevated probability window for the next great earthquake.

**Recurrence regularity for full-fault ruptures**: A striking pattern emerges when considering only through-going great earthquakes that rupture the entire SAF system. The three-event sequence (~1285 → ~1580 → 1857) yields intervals of 295 and 277 years, respectively—a mean recurrence of 286 ± 9 years with a coefficient of variation (CV) of only 0.03. This remarkably low CV suggests quasi-periodic behavior for full-fault SAF ruptures, in contrast to the higher variability seen when segment ruptures are included (Table S1). If this pattern holds, the next full-fault rupture would be expected circa 2143 CE (286 years after 1857). However, this projection rests on only two inter-event intervals—a sample size too small for robust statistical inference.

**Critical caveats**:
1. The ~1285 and ~1580 events have PROBABLE status, not CONFIRMED
2. Wide radiocarbon windows cannot prove synchrony of the ~1285 event components
3. Recurrence intervals show high variance (σ ≈ 57 years based on documented events)
4. The 1825 event appears localized and should not be included in full-fault recurrence calculations

We emphasize that the current evidence warrants further paleoseismic investigation, not immediate revision of hazard models. The candidate events should not be incorporated into seismic hazard assessments until independently confirmed through high-precision dating.

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 The Power of Multi-Proxy Convergence

No single archive can prove synchronous fault rupture across 800 km. Radiocarbon dating lacks the temporal precision; tree rings lack spatial coverage; turbidites lack onshore correlation. But when five independent archives—representing different physical systems, different geographic locations, and different dating methods—all converge on the same narrow time window, the probability of coincidence becomes negligible.

The ~1285 CE event exemplifies this principle. The 32-year overlap window (1290–1322 CE) emerges from:
- Pallett Creek stratigraphy (physical rupture horizon)
- Vedanta Marsh trenching (ground deformation)
- Carrizo Plain paleoseismology (fault offset)
- Noyo turbidites (submarine landslide)
- Archaeological collapse (cultural disruption)

These are not five measurements of the same phenomenon—they are five independent systems that would only show simultaneous perturbation if a single great earthquake affected the entire region. The convergence constitutes strong evidence for a ~1285 CE through-going SAF rupture.

### 5.2 Implications for UCERF3

The Uniform California Earthquake Rupture Forecast exhibits systematic misfits to paleoseismic observations on the southern SAF[25,26]. UCERF3's branch-averaged slip rates underpredict paleoseismic event counts, suggesting either missed earthquakes or underestimated magnitudes.

Our findings offer a potential resolution: if the ~1285 CE and ~1580 CE events are real, UCERF3 is missing earthquakes in its calibration dataset. The revised recurrence of 156–189 years would bring model predictions into better agreement with paleoseismic constraints.

However, we caution against premature model revision. The candidate events require independent confirmation through:
1. High-precision dendrochronological dating of the 1285 window
2. Additional paleoseismic trenching at intermediate sites
3. Search for corroborating evidence in lake sediments (Pallett Creek lacustrine sequence, Lake Tahoe turbidites)
4. Archival research for 1825 CE historical documentation

### 5.3 Limitations

**Dating precision**: The ~1285 CE event window (1257–1322 CE) spans 65 years. While five-way convergence strongly suggests synchrony, we cannot rule out the alternative hypothesis that North and South SAF segments ruptured in separate events decades apart.

**Tree ring anchoring**: The Carroll et al. (2025) chronologies have secure crossdating only after 1457 CE. Extension to the ~1285 CE window requires crossdating against ITRDB reference chronologies (Sillett et al. 2014), which has not yet been completed.

**Validation transferability**: Our Cascadia validation demonstrates 53% detection of M8+ megathrusts at 90 km distance. The SAF is a strike-slip system with different stress transfer characteristics; detection efficiency may differ.

**Single speleothem**: Oregon Caves represents one cave with one stalagmite. Replication with additional Pacific Northwest caves would strengthen validation.

### 5.4 Quantitative Validation Through Hydrogeochemical Modeling

The Chiodini hydrogeochemical model[7,8] provides quantitative predictions that can be tested against observed speleothem anomalies. For detected events, modeling predicts CO₂ flux perturbations of +130% to +1000% at cave sites, with recovery durations of 4.7–35.6 years—consistent with observed anomaly lengths of 5–46 years[29]. Peak ground accelerations (PGA) calculated using the Bindi et al. (2011) ground motion prediction equation[30] exceed 0.05g (MMI ≥ VI) for all detected events.

Seismic energy density calculations using the Wang & Manga (2010) framework[28] reveal a detection threshold of approximately **15 J/m³**. Events above this threshold (1896 Independence M6.3 at 48 km: 8.7 J/m³; Cascadia megathrusts at 90 km: >20 J/m³) are reliably detected, while events below 1 J/m³ (1952 Kern County M7.3 at 178 km: 0.9 J/m³) produce no signal regardless of magnitude. This energy density threshold, combined with the static strain distance constraint (Section 2.1.1), explains why distant large earthquakes go undetected while nearby moderate earthquakes produce strong signals—the methodology responds to permanent aquifer deformation rather than transient ground shaking.

### 5.5 The 8.2 ka Event: Climatic Baseline Validation

A critical test of seismic-climatic discrimination is whether major Holocene climate events produce signals that could be misidentified as earthquakes. The 8.2 kiloyear event (~6200 BCE) provides an ideal test case—the most pronounced cold anomaly of the Holocene, with global expression in ice cores, ocean sediments, and terrestrial archives[31].

The Oregon Caves record captures this event with remarkable clarity (Table 6).

**Table 6: 8.2 ka climatic event vs. T11 seismic event signatures**

| Parameter | 8.2 ka Event (~6200–6000 BCE) | T11 Megathrust (~4050 BCE) |
|-----------|-------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Peak z-score | -1.0 to -1.4σ | **-3.3 to -4.0σ** |
| Duration | ~200 years | ~10–20 years |
| Pattern | Diffuse, gradual | Sharp spike, rapid recovery |
| Anomaly count | 0 samples at z > 2.0 | **16 samples at z > 2.0** |

The 8.2 ka event—despite being the strongest Holocene climate perturbation—produces only mild δ18O excursions (z = -1.0 to -1.4σ) spread over ~200 years. By contrast, the T11 Cascadia megathrust generates extreme excursions (z = -3.3 to -4.0σ) concentrated in a ~10–20 year window. The order-of-magnitude differences in both magnitude and duration confirm that climatic events, even extreme ones, do not mimic seismic signatures.

This baseline establishes three diagnostic criteria for seismic discrimination:
1. **Magnitude threshold**: Seismic events produce z > 2.0σ; major climate events stay below z = 1.5σ
2. **Duration constraint**: Seismic anomalies resolve in <50 years; climatic events persist for centuries
3. **Pattern recognition**: Seismic signals show sharp onset (sawtooth); climatic signals show gradual transitions

These criteria provide confidence that the Cascadia detections (T5, Event S, Event W) represent genuine seismic perturbations rather than paleoclimatic artifacts.

## 6. Conclusions

1. **Cascadia validation**: Speleothem paleoseismology detects independently-dated megathrust earthquakes at 10.7× above random chance (8/15 events, P < 0.00001), establishing the methodology's validity for great earthquake detection.

2. **San Andreas discovery**: Five independent archives converge on a previously undocumented ~1285 CE rupture, with a 32-year overlap window (1290–1322 CE). The probability of chance convergence is ~7.8 × 10⁻⁷. Tree ring analysis reveals additional candidates at ~1580 CE and ~1825 CE.

3. **Recurrence implications**: If confirmed, the dark earthquakes imply SAF recurrence intervals of 156–189 years—25–40% shorter than the ~250 years assumed in UCERF3. The southern SAF would currently be at 89–108% of the revised mean cycle.

4. **Hazard significance**: These findings warrant urgent paleoseismic investigation of the ~1285 CE event through high-precision dating methods. Confirmation would require substantial revision of California seismic hazard models.

5. **Methodological advance**: Multi-proxy convergence—combining speleothems, tree rings, turbidites, trenching, and archaeology—provides a framework for resolving events that no single archive can constrain.

The existence of "dark earthquakes" missing from both historical and paleoseismic catalogs challenges our confidence in recurrence-based hazard assessment. The San Andreas Fault may be more dangerous than current models indicate.
